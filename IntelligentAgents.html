Unit 1: Introduction to Agent-Based Computing

Overview

Week 1 explored the trends in computing that have led to the rise of agent-based computing. The concept of an agent was introduced, along with its growing relevance across various sectors and applications.

The lecturecast focused on the drivers of intelligent computing, emphasizing agent-based approaches. It examined the autonomy of computer programs and their significance in the current technological landscape.

  • Identified the trends that contributed to the growth in agent-based technologies.
  • Analyzed the implications of these trends on the broader computing landscape.
  • Explored agent-environment interactions and agent-to-agent communication.
  • Presented examples of agent-based systems and their applications.

Key Learning

  • Developed a foundational understanding of agent-based computing concepts.
  • Recognized the computing trends that support the agent paradigm.
  • Compared different agent-based systems to evaluate their strengths and limitations.

Collaborative Discussion 1: Initial Post by Marwa Alkuwari

The emergence of agent-based systems (ABS) as a prominent computational paradigm stemmed from the increasing complexity of modern challenges and the limitations of centralized systems. ABS, consisting of autonomous agents capable of decision-making and interaction, gained traction due to advancements in AI and the rise of distributed computing environments (Wooldridge, 2009).

Key drivers included the spread of IoT and cloud computing, progress in multi-agent systems (MAS) theory, and the demand for real-time solutions in sectors like logistics, healthcare, and finance. These systems offered enhanced efficiency through parallelism, greater system resilience, and the ability to simulate complex scenarios (Bonabeau, 2002; Jennings, 1998).

Unit 2: Introducing First Order Logic

Overview

Week 2 focused on the foundational elements of First Order Logic (FOL), also known as Predicate Logic. The seminar introduced students to the core mathematical structures that underpin intelligent systems and logical reasoning in AI.

  • Explored key terms and symbols used in first order logic.
  • Demonstrated how FOL relates to natural language expressions.
  • Discussed the role of universal and existential quantifiers.

Key Learning

  • Understood core elements such as predicates, constants, variables, functions, and logical connectives.
  • Converted natural language statements into formal FOL notation.
  • Applied universal (∀) and existential (∃) quantifiers in logical expressions.
  • Recognized how FOL supports structured knowledge representation in AI.

Collaborative Discussion 2: Peer Response by Noora Alboinin

Noora highlighted that ABS mirror the modern need for distributed, scalable, and flexible solutions. She emphasized their strength in simulating emergent behaviors, which proves beneficial in domains such as urban planning and finance (Jennings, 1998). However, she also pointed out challenges like governance complexity and ethical implications in autonomous decision-making, reinforcing the need for legal and supervisory frameworks (Bonabeau, 2002).
