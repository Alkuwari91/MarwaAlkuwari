Unit 1: Introduction to Agent-Based Computing

Overview

Welcome to Week 1. We will be setting the agenda by looking at the trends in computing that have led to the rise of agent-based computing. We will also introduce the concept of an agent, what it is and why they have gained popularity across a breadth of sectors and applications.

This week’s lecturecast will focus on the drivers of growth of intelligent computing with a specific focus on agent-based approaches. It will discuss what it means for a computer program to be autonomous, and why that is relevant in today’s technological landscape.

In this unit we shall:

  • Look at the trends in computing that have led to the growth in agent-based technologies.
  • Investigate the implications of these growing trends and their impact on the wider landscape.
  • Explore agents and how they can interact with the environment and each other.
  • Define examples of agent-based systems and their applications.

Key Learning

  • Develop the understanding of key concepts of agent-based computing.
  • Understand the trends that have facilitated the development of agents as a paradigm.
  • Compare different types of agent-based system to contrast their relative merits.

Collaborative Discussion 1: Agent Based Systems

Summary of Key Points:

  • Why ABS? - They address the limitations of centralized systems in dynamic, distributed environments.
  • Core Features: - Autonomy, adaptability, interaction, and scalability.
  • Main Drivers:
    • Growth of distributed systems (IoT, cloud computing)
    • Advances in Multi-Agent Systems (MAS)
    • Organizational demand for real-time responsiveness
  • Benefits:
    • Improved efficiency through parallelism
    • Resilience and fault tolerance
    • Simulation of complex, real-world systems
  • Challenges:
    • Implementation and coordination complexity
    • Ethical concerns in autonomous decision-making
    • Need for legal and oversight frameworks
  • Conclusion: ABS are a powerful, transformative tool for modern systems, but require responsible integration and governance to unlock their full potential.

Unit 2: Introducing First Order Logic

Overview

Welcome to Week 2. During this week we have the first seminar for the module and will focus on discussing the fundamental elements of First Order Logic (also known as Predicate Logic). Students will engage with the mathematical notations that underpin this logic system to better understand key elements relevant to intelligent agents and wider AI topics.

In this unit we shall:

  • Cover the key terms and symbols used in first order logic.
  • Demonstrate the relationship between first order logic and natural language.
  • Discuss the use of quantifiers within first order logic.

Key Learning

  • Understand the core elements of first order logic, including predicates, constants, variables, functions, and logical connectives.
  • Gain the ability to represent natural language statements in formal first order logic notation.
  • Apply universal (∀) and existential (∃) quantifiers in logical reasoning.
  • Explore how first order logic contributes to knowledge representation in AI.

Collaborative Discussion 2: First Order Logic

Summary of Key Points:

  • What is First Order Logic (FOL)? - A formal system used to express statements, relationships, and rules with precision in AI.
  • Components of FOL: - Includes predicates, quantifiers, variables, constants, and connectives (∧, ∨, →, ¬).
  • Why is FOL Important in AI?
    • Provides a foundation for representing complex knowledge structures.
    • Enables automated reasoning and inference making.
    • Bridges human language and machine logic.
  • Applications: - Used in intelligent systems to understand rules, make decisions, and derive new information.
  • Reference: Russell, S. & Norvig, P. (2021) Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach, 4th ed., Chapter 8.

Unit 3: Correlation and Regression

In this unit, I deepened my understanding of how statistical relationships between variables can be identified and modeled using different types of regression. We covered simple, multiple, and polynomial regression, each of which supports the predictive capabilities central to AI and data science. These techniques help us recognize dependencies such as how age, education level, or income might be interlinked.

Learning Highlights:

  • Understanding Relationships: I developed the ability to measure the strength and direction of relationships between variables using correlation analysis.
  • Predictive Modeling: I practiced building linear models to forecast outcomes based on input variables. This process involved identifying when to apply simple versus more complex regression models.
  • Ethical and Practical Insight: We critically reflected on the broader impacts of data-driven predictions—particularly the ethical and legal consequences of using personal or sensitive data in predictive systems. Ensuring fairness, transparency, and inclusiveness in model development became a key discussion point.
  • Data Challenges: We addressed real-world complexities like data diversity, noise, and overfitting, which require careful data preparation and variable selection.

Theory into Practice:

Regression models form part of intelligent agents' reasoning abilities, akin to how Maes' Agent Network Architecture (ANA) enables autonomous decision-making. Just as modules in ANA compete based on activation energy to influence behavior, variables in a regression model influence predicted outcomes based on their weights. Both processes reflect adaptive systems that evolve with context and input data.

Drawing inspiration from Maes (1991), regression analysis can be viewed as one layer of reasoning in an intelligent system—supporting the agent’s goal-achieving behavior by identifying relevant patterns. This aligns with the broader concept of agents learning from feedback and adapting over time, as seen in competence modules learning which actions lead to successful outcomes.

References:

  • Maes, P. (1991). The Agent Network Architecture (ANA). ACM SIGART Bulletin.
  • Bratman, M. E., Israel, D. J., & Pollack, M. E. (1988). Plans and Resource-Bounded Practical Reasoning. Computational Intelligence.
  • Kaelbling, L. P. (1991). A Situated-Automata Approach to the Design of Embedded Agents. ACM SIGART Bulletin.
  • Brooks, R. A. (1991). Intelligence Without Representation. Artificial Intelligence.
  • Reynolds, C. W. (1987). Flocks, Herds and Schools: A Distributed Behavioral Model. ACM SIGGRAPH Computer Graphics.

Unit 4: Linear Regression with Scikit-Learn

This unit focused on applying linear regression techniques using Python’s Scikit-Learn library. I explored how to model relationships between variables in both simple and multivariate contexts. The learning extended to evaluating model performance using key metrics such as R-squared, allowing for better judgment of prediction accuracy and model reliability.

As datasets grow in complexity, understanding how to optimize models becomes essential. This unit emphasized the practical challenges of modeling real-world data, from selecting meaningful features to adjusting model parameters for improved results.

Learning Highlights:

  • Applying Regression to Complex Datasets: I developed the ability to model both straightforward and multivariable datasets using linear regression, identifying patterns and trends that support data-driven decision-making.
  • Model Evaluation and Optimization: I practiced refining regression models using evaluation metrics to improve prediction accuracy. Understanding how to tune model parameters was a key takeaway.
  • Proficiency in Scikit-Learn: I enhanced my technical skills in using Scikit-Learn’s regression tools, streamlining the process of model creation, training, and evaluation in real-time scenarios.

Theory in Context:

In the context of intelligent systems, predictive modeling plays a vital role in how agents interpret environmental inputs and forecast outcomes. Drawing from Wooldridge (2009), especially Chapter 5, Section 5.2, an agent's ability to make informed predictions aligns with the need for models like linear regression in adaptive decision-making.

This reflects a larger principle in multiagent systems: intelligent agents must reason with uncertainty and learn from data. Regression analysis provides a foundation for agents to adaptively respond based on predictive insights, whether in simulations, financial systems, or healthcare models.

Seminar Summary: Regression with Scikit-Learn

In this hands-on seminar, we practiced implementing simple and multiple regression models using Python in Google Colab. We learned how to apply, test, and optimize our models through practical exercises, gaining a deeper understanding of predictive modeling in real applications.

Unit 5: Clustering

This unit focused on the principles and applications of clustering, a fundamental technique in unsupervised learning. Clustering involves organizing data points into groups based on similarity, enabling patterns to emerge without predefined labels. This approach is widely used across domains such as bioinformatics, market segmentation, image recognition, and natural language processing.

We examined multiple clustering strategies, including K-means and agglomerative clustering, learning how to evaluate their effectiveness using internal validation techniques such as inertia and silhouette scores. Additionally, we discussed distance metrics like Euclidean and Manhattan distances, which are critical for determining similarity between data points.

Learning Highlights:

  • Core Concepts of Clustering: I gained a foundational understanding of how clustering works and why it is useful in revealing hidden structures within data.
  • Applying and Evaluating Algorithms: I practiced implementing K-means and agglomerative clustering, and assessed their outputs using metrics that evaluate the cohesiveness and separation of clusters.
  • Ethical and Technical Challenges: I reflected on the limitations and potential biases of clustering techniques—especially the risks when applying unsupervised models to complex, real-world datasets.

Theory in Context:

Clustering aligns closely with foundational ideas in communication and knowledge representation within multi-agent systems. Drawing from Searle’s (1969) theory of speech acts, the categorization of data can be seen as a form of "assertive" act—where agents attempt to describe the world based on observed similarities.

Similarly, the work of Payne and Tamma (2014) on negotiating ontologies in distributed systems relates directly to clustering. In scenarios where agents must reconcile differing views of a domain (i.e., their own “ontologies”), clustering techniques can assist in aligning and matching concepts based on shared attributes—despite incomplete or asymmetric knowledge.

This connection enhances the conceptual value of clustering in AI: not just as a tool for statistical grouping, but also as a cognitive mechanism for knowledge construction and communication among intelligent agents.

References:

  • Searle, J.R. (1969). Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language. Cambridge University Press.
  • Payne, T.R. & Tamma, V. (2014). Negotiating over Ontological Correspondences with Asymmetric and Incomplete Knowledge. Journal of Web Semantics.
