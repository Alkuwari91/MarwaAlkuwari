Unit 1: Introduction to Agent-Based Computing

Overview

Week 1 explored the trends in computing that have led to the rise of agent-based computing. The concept of an agent was introduced, along with its growing relevance across various sectors and applications.

The lecturecast focused on the drivers of intelligent computing, emphasizing agent-based approaches. It examined the autonomy of computer programs and their significance in the current technological landscape.

  • Identified the trends that contributed to the growth in agent-based technologies.
  • Analyzed the implications of these trends on the broader computing landscape.
  • Explored agent-environment interactions and agent-to-agent communication.
  • Presented examples of agent-based systems and their applications.

Key Learning

  • Developed a foundational understanding of agent-based computing concepts.
  • Recognized the computing trends that support the agent paradigm.
  • Compared different agent-based systems to evaluate their strengths and limitations.

Collaborative Discussion 1: Agent Based Systems

Initial Post by Marwa Alkuwari: Agent-based systems (ABS) emerged as a solution to the growing complexity of contemporary problems and the shortcomings of centralized approaches. These systems rely on autonomous agents capable of decision-making and interaction, empowered by developments in AI and distributed computing (Wooldridge, 2009). Driving factors include the rise of IoT and cloud technologies, advances in multi-agent systems (Jennings, 1998), and increased demand for real-time adaptability across industries such as logistics, healthcare, and finance. ABS provide benefits like parallel processing, increased resilience, and simulation capabilities that support innovation and strategic decision-making (Bonabeau, 2002).

References

  • Bonabeau, E. (2002) ‘Agent-based modeling: Methods and techniques for simulating human systems’, Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 99(3), pp. 7280–7287.
  • Jennings, N.R. (1998) ‘Agent-oriented software engineering’, Artificial Intelligence, 100(1-2), pp. 277–296.
  • Wooldridge, M. (2009) An Introduction to MultiAgent Systems. 2nd edn. Chichester: Wiley.

Collaborative Discussion 2: Agent Communication Languages

Summary of Initial Post by Marwa Alkuwari: This post compared Agent Communication Languages (ACLs), such as KQML, with traditional method invocation. ACLs were highlighted for their high-level, intent-based communication using performatives, which enables dynamic coordination and knowledge sharing across heterogeneous agents. These capabilities are underpinned by Speech Act Theory and promote semantic-rich interaction, unlike the rigid, syntactic nature of method invocation in languages like Python or Java. However, ACLs also introduce complexity due to the need for shared ontologies and parsing mechanisms. The post concluded that while ACLs are ideal for flexible and distributed systems, method invocation remains efficient for tightly coupled, performance-driven contexts.

Collaborative Discussion 2: Agent Communication Languages – Summary Post

Summary by Marwa Alkuwari: This post wrapped up the exploration of Agent Communication Languages (ACLs), with a specific focus on KQML. Marwa highlighted the flexibility and semantic richness of KQML through performatives like "tell" and "ask", which make agent intent and context explicit (Finin et al., 1994). These features allow for seamless interoperability between agents, particularly when using standards such as FIPA ACL (De Ridder, 2025). In contrast, method invocation in languages like Java or Python lacks native mechanisms to convey context or purpose, relying instead on hard-coded instructions.

Despite KQML’s strengths, it brings implementation complexity due to its reliance on shared ontologies and complex parsing mechanisms (Labrou, Finin, & Peng, 1999). Rodrigo supported this by emphasizing KQML's strength in reasoning and dynamic knowledge exchange (Chalupsky et al., 1993; Mayfield et al., 1996). Together, the discussion concluded that while KQML may not match the performance of simpler invocation methods, it plays a valuable role in building intelligent, semantically aware agents.
